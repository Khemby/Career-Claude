# Skill: Resume Customization

## Purpose
Use this skill to tailor a resume to a specific job description. Apply keyword matching, content reordering, bullet rewriting, and summary customization to maximize relevance and ATS match score.

---

## Why Customization Is Non-Negotiable

A generic resume is optimized for no one. A tailored resume speaks directly to what this hiring team is looking for right now. Candidates who customize their resume for each role are significantly more likely to pass ATS filters and get callbacks.

The goal: your resume should feel like it was written *for this role*, not adapted to it.

---

## Step 1: Analyze the Job Description

Before touching the resume, extract and organize information from the JD.

### JD Deconstruction Checklist

**1. Required vs. Preferred Skills**
- Mark each requirement as "Required" (must-have) or "Preferred" (nice-to-have)
- Required items must appear on the resume. Preferred items should appear if the candidate has them.

**2. Keyword Categories**
Extract keywords by type:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Technical skills** | Python, SQL, Salesforce, Google Analytics, AutoCAD |
| **Methodologies** | Agile, Scrum, Lean, Six Sigma, OKRs |
| **Soft skills mentioned** | Cross-functional collaboration, executive communication |
| **Domain terms** | B2B SaaS, enterprise sales, clinical trials, P&L ownership |
| **Job title variations** | What do they call this role? What level? |

**3. Frequency Analysis**
Any term appearing 3+ times in the JD is critical. It must appear in the resume.

**4. Company Signals**
Read the "About Us" and "What We're Looking For" sections for tone and culture signals. Match your language to theirs (e.g., if they say "builders", use "built" in your bullets).

**5. Success Metrics**
What does success look like in this role? Any mention of metrics or goals tells you what bullets to emphasize.

### Values-Driven Customization

Keyword matching gets you past the ATS. Values alignment is what makes a hiring manager think "this person belongs here."

**Go beyond the requirements section.** Read the company's "About Us," mission statement, and the tone of the JD itself. These reveal what the organization actually cares about — and your resume should reflect those signals back.

**How to apply it:**

1. **Identify value signals** — If the company emphasizes "innovation," surface your most creative or entrepreneurial work. If they talk about "impact," lead with your biggest measurable outcomes. If they say "collaboration," foreground cross-functional wins.
2. **Match their language naturally** — If the JD says "builders," use "built" in your bullets. If they say "data-driven culture," lead your achievements with metrics. If they talk about "ownership," describe initiatives you drove end-to-end.
3. **Lead with resonant stories** — If you've done self-discovery work and know your top professional values, check whether they overlap with the company's stated values. When they do, the experiences that demonstrate those shared values should be the first bullets the reader sees.
4. **Reflect culture in tone** — A startup that calls itself "scrappy" wants concise, high-energy language. An enterprise company emphasizing "rigor" wants precise, structured descriptions. Let the JD's voice inform yours.

**Important:** This is not about being fake. You are not inventing alignment — you are choosing which true stories to tell based on what will resonate most with this specific reader. Every candidate has dozens of real accomplishments; values-driven customization is the art of selecting and sequencing the right ones.

## Step 2: Gap Analysis

Compare the analyzed JD to the existing resume.

### Gap Analysis Table
Create a simple comparison:

| JD Requirement | On Resume? | Action Needed |
|---------------|-----------|---------------|
| [skill/requirement] | Yes / Partially / No | Add / Emphasize / Reframe / Skip |

### Gap Categories

**Hard Gaps** — Candidate genuinely lacks the requirement
- If a hard gap is a "required" skill, flag it. The candidate may not be a fit, or needs to address it directly.
- If it's a "preferred" skill, note the gap but proceed with customization.

**Soft Gaps** — Candidate has the experience but hasn't expressed it the right way
- This is the most common case. The candidate did the work but called it something different.
- Fix: reframe existing bullets using JD language.

**Hidden Experience** — Candidate has the skill buried in context the JD cares about
- Example: JD asks for "stakeholder management" — candidate has it buried in a project management bullet.
- Fix: extract and surface this experience more prominently.

### The 60% Rule

Don't let a gap analysis talk you out of applying. Research from Harvard Business Review shows that men typically apply when they meet roughly 60% of listed qualifications, while women often wait until they meet nearly 100%. The result is a confidence gap, not a competence gap.

**The reality:** Job descriptions are wishlists, not checklists. Hiring managers write aspirational JDs knowing they'll rarely find a candidate who checks every box. Companies will invest in training and ramp time when the core fit is strong.

**The guideline:** If you meet 60% or more of the requirements, apply. Frame the gaps as growth areas in your cover letter or summary — not as disqualifiers. Even "required" skills are rarely absolute dealbreakers for a candidate who demonstrates strong fundamentals, fast learning, and clear alignment with the role's core mission.

## Step 3: Keyword Integration

### Rules for Keyword Placement
1. **Never add a keyword you can't back up** — ATS gets you past the filter, but the human reader will ask about it
2. **Integrate naturally** — Keywords should appear in context, not stuffed into a skills list awkwardly
3. **Mirror exact phrasing** — If the JD says "revenue operations", don't say "rev ops" or "sales operations" — use their exact phrase
4. **Distribute keywords across sections** — Summary, bullets, and skills section all carry weight

### Where to Add Keywords

| Section | Best For |
|---------|---------|
| **Summary** | 2–3 of the most critical keywords for immediate context |
| **Skills section** | Tools, technologies, certifications, methodologies |
| **Bullet points** | Skills demonstrated in context (highest credibility) |
| **Job titles** | If the candidate's actual title differs from industry standard, add a "translation" in parentheses if honest |

## Step 4: Bullet Rewriting

### Rewriting Framework

**Step 1**: Identify which existing bullets are closest to JD requirements.

**Step 2**: For each relevant bullet, ask:
- Does it use the same terminology as the JD?
- Does it emphasize the outcome the JD cares about?
- Is the context clear to someone in this industry?

**Step 3**: Rewrite using this formula:
**[JD Keyword / Action Verb] + [Specific What You Did] + [Result in Terms the JD Cares About]**

### Before/After Examples

**JD requires**: "Experience managing enterprise client relationships and driving retention"

Before:
> "Worked with large accounts to resolve issues and keep them satisfied"

After:
> "Managed portfolio of 12 enterprise accounts ($4.2M ARR), driving 96% annual retention through quarterly business reviews and proactive escalation resolution"

---

**JD requires**: "Cross-functional collaboration with engineering and product teams"

Before:
> "Collaborated with other departments on product launches"

After:
> "Partnered with engineering and product teams across 3 time zones to deliver 8 feature releases on schedule, reducing average launch cycle by 18%"

---

**JD requires**: "Data-driven decision making and comfort with SQL / BI tools"

Before:
> "Used data to inform marketing decisions"

After:
> "Leveraged SQL and Looker to build campaign performance dashboards, enabling data-driven budget reallocation that improved ROAS by 34%"

---

### Bullet Rewriting Priorities
1. First 3 bullets under most recent/relevant role — these get the most attention
2. Bullets that directly address "required" JD items
3. Summary — should be completely rewritten for each application

### Addressing Concerns Proactively

The best resumes answer objections before the reader forms them. If you think a hiring manager might wonder about something, address it — don't hope they won't notice.

**Career Changers**
- Lead with transferable skills, not your previous job title. If you managed complex projects in healthcare and you're moving into tech, "project management" is the headline — not "hospital administrator."
- Frame the switch as intentional growth, not a retreat. Connect the old function to the new one: "Applied data analysis skills honed in clinical research to product analytics" reads as a deliberate evolution.
- Your cross-industry perspective is an asset. Name it explicitly in your summary.

**Employment Gaps**
- Brief, honest, one sentence. "Took parental leave (2023–2024)" or "Completed full-stack development bootcamp" or "Managed family health matter — now fully available and energized to return."
- No apology, no over-explanation. Gaps are normal. The resume's job is to show what you can do, not account for every month.
- If you did anything professionally relevant during the gap (freelance, volunteering, coursework), include it.

**Overqualification**
- Don't hide your experience — explain why this role excites you. A brief line in the summary works: "Seeking a focused IC role after 5 years in management to return to hands-on engineering."
- Emphasize the specific aspects of this scope that appeal to you. Hiring managers worry you'll be bored; show them you won't be.

**Underqualification**
- Highlight adjacent experience and pattern-match to JD requirements. "Haven't used Kubernetes" becomes "Managed containerized deployments in Docker; currently completing Kubernetes certification."
- Show a trajectory of rapid learning. If you picked up three new tools in your last role, that pattern matters more than any single missing skill.
- Quantify your ramp-up speed when possible: "Self-taught Python in 6 weeks to automate team reporting."

**The principle:** If you think they'll wonder, answer it on the resume. A proactive one-liner beats an awkward silence that lets the reader fill in their own (usually worse) story.

## Step 5: Section Reordering

### Logic for Reordering

**Move the most relevant experience up.** If the candidate's most recent role isn't their most relevant, consider:
- Pulling the most relevant role up (only if dates allow — don't imply it's current)
- Adding a "Relevant Experience" vs. "Additional Experience" structure
- Featuring a "Selected Projects" or "Key Achievements" section near the top

**Reorder bullet points within each role.** The most relevant bullets go first, not necessarily the most recent tasks.

**Skills section placement:**
- For technical roles where skills are screened first: put Skills near the top (after summary)
- For non-technical roles: Skills can remain lower on the page

## Step 6: Summary Rewrite

The summary should be completely rewritten for each Tier A application. It is the first thing read and should make the hiring manager think "this person gets exactly what we need."

### Customized Summary Formula
**[Their preferred job title] with [X years] in [their industry/domain]. Specialized in [2–3 of their highest-priority requirements]. Proven track record of [outcome language from JD]. Looking to [what you'll bring to their specific context].**

### Example

JD: Senior Product Manager at a B2B SaaS company focused on enterprise growth and reducing time-to-value for new customers.

Before (generic):
> "Experienced Product Manager with a track record of launching successful products. Strong communicator with a collaborative approach."

After (tailored):
> "Senior Product Manager with 7 years in B2B SaaS, specializing in enterprise onboarding and time-to-value optimization. Led 0→1 launches for 4 platform features adopted by Fortune 500 clients. Known for bridging engineering, sales, and customer success to deliver products that reduce churn and accelerate expansion revenue."

---

## Step 7: Final Checklist

Before submitting the customized resume:

- [ ] All "required" keywords from JD appear on the resume
- [ ] Most "preferred" keywords appear where the candidate legitimately has them
- [ ] Summary references the specific role type and top 2–3 JD requirements
- [ ] Most relevant bullets are listed first within each role
- [ ] Every claim is backed by a specific example or metric
- [ ] No keywords were added that the candidate cannot speak to in an interview
- [ ] Section headings use standard language (ATS safe)
- [ ] File is saved as PDF (unless otherwise specified)
- [ ] No formatting elements that would break ATS parsing (no tables, columns, text boxes)
- [ ] Length is appropriate (1 page if <10 years exp, 2 pages max otherwise)

## Customization Time Investment Guide

| Application Tier | Time | What to Customize |
|-----------------|------|------------------|
| **Tier A** (dream role) | 2–3 hours | Full rewrite of summary, all bullets in top 2 roles, skills section review |
| **Tier B** (good fit) | 45–90 min | Summary rewrite, top 3–5 bullets per role, skills section |
| **Tier C** (exploratory) | 20–30 min | Summary swap, keyword check, critical gap fixes only |
