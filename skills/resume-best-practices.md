# Skill: Resume Best Practices

## Purpose
Use this skill to audit a resume for structure, content quality, ATS compatibility, and impact. Apply these frameworks to identify problems and deliver specific, actionable feedback.

---

## Resume Fundamentals

### Format Rules
- **Length**: 1 page for under 10 years experience; 2 pages maximum for senior roles. Never exceed 2 pages.
- **Font**: Single professional font (Calibri, Garamond, Georgia, or Arial). 10–12pt body, 14–16pt name.
- **Margins**: 0.5"–1" on all sides. Consistent spacing throughout.
- **File type**: PDF (preserves formatting). Some ATS systems require .docx — note this when relevant.
- **Columns**: Avoid multi-column layouts — most ATS parsers read left-to-right, top-to-bottom and fail on columns.
- **Tables, text boxes, headers/footers**: Avoid — ATS systems cannot parse these reliably.
- **Color**: Minimal. Black body text. Subtle accent color for headers is acceptable.
- **Graphics/icons**: Avoid skill bars, rating circles, or icons — ATS ignores them and they look unprofessional.

### Required Sections (in priority order)
1. **Contact Information** — Name, city/state, phone, professional email, LinkedIn URL, GitHub (if technical)
2. **Professional Summary** — 2–4 sentences targeting the specific role type
3. **Work Experience** — Reverse chronological. Company, title, dates, location, bullets.
4. **Skills** — Technical and domain-specific skills. Not soft skills.
5. **Education** — Degree, school, year. GPA only if 3.5+ and within 3 years of graduation.

### Optional Sections
- Certifications (high value — list prominently)
- Projects (essential for early-career or career changers)
- Volunteer work (only if relevant to target role)
- Publications / Patents (for research, academic, or technical roles)

## Writing Strong Bullet Points

### The Formula
**[Action Verb] + [What You Did] + [How / With What] + [Result / Impact]**

Example:
- Weak: "Responsible for managing social media accounts"
- Strong: "Grew Instagram following 340% in 6 months by launching a weekly short-form video series, increasing inbound leads by 28%"

### Action Verb Tiers

**Tier 1 — Leadership & Impact** (use for senior/leadership bullets)
Led, Directed, Spearheaded, Launched, Established, Transformed, Championed, Drove

**Tier 2 — Execution & Delivery** (use for core responsibilities)
Built, Developed, Designed, Implemented, Delivered, Created, Produced, Engineered

**Tier 3 — Collaboration & Process** (use sparingly)
Partnered, Collaborated, Supported, Coordinated, Facilitated

**Avoid entirely:**
Helped, Assisted, Worked on, Participated in, Responsible for, Managed various, Handled

### Quantification Guide
Always push for numbers. Use these categories:
- **Scale**: team size, budget managed, users/customers served, data volume
- **Growth**: % increase in revenue, traffic, engagement, retention
- **Efficiency**: time saved, cost reduced, error rate decreased
- **Output**: features shipped, campaigns run, reports delivered, deals closed

If exact numbers aren't available, use ranges or approximations: "~$2M budget", "15–20 stakeholders", "3x faster than previous process"

### Bullet Point Checklist
- [ ] Starts with a strong action verb (past tense for previous roles, present tense for current)
- [ ] Contains at least one quantified result
- [ ] Is 1–2 lines maximum
- [ ] Does not start with "I"
- [ ] Avoids buzzwords: "synergy", "results-driven", "detail-oriented", "team player"
- [ ] Is specific enough that only you could have written it

## ATS Compatibility

### How ATS Works
Applicant Tracking Systems scan resumes for keywords from the job description and rank candidates by match score. A resume that gets a low ATS score may never be seen by a human, regardless of quality.

### ATS Rules
1. **Use standard section headings**: "Work Experience" not "My Journey" or "Where I've Been"
2. **Spell out acronyms once**: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" then use "SEO" freely
3. **Mirror the JD language exactly**: If the JD says "cross-functional collaboration", your resume should say "cross-functional collaboration" — not "working across teams"
4. **Include a Skills section**: ATS systems parse this section heavily. List tools, technologies, methodologies.
5. **No images, charts, or infographics**: They are invisible to ATS
6. **Avoid headers/footers for contact info**: ATS often skips these

### Keyword Extraction Method
When analyzing a JD, look for:
- **Job title variations** (what do they call the role?)
- **Required tools/technologies** (software, platforms, languages)
- **Methodologies** (Agile, Scrum, Six Sigma, etc.)
- **Credentials/certifications** mentioned
- **Repeated phrases** (if a skill appears 3+ times, it's critical)

## AI-Era Resume Positioning

### How ATS Has Evolved
Traditional ATS used simple keyword matching — count the matches, rank the candidates. Modern systems go further:
- **Semantic analysis**: AI-powered ATS can recognize synonyms and related concepts. "Led engineering team" and "managed developers" may score similarly. This helps, but exact JD phrasing still wins in tiebreakers.
- **Pattern recognition**: Some systems evaluate career trajectory, job tenure patterns, and role progression — not just keywords.
- **Contextual scoring**: Advanced platforms weigh where a keyword appears (a skill in your job title or summary carries more weight than the same skill buried in bullet #7 of a previous role).

Bottom line: keyword matching still matters, but the systems are smarter. Write for meaning, not just for stuffing terms in.

### Skills-Based Hiring
A growing number of companies now evaluate candidates on **skills and demonstrated abilities** rather than credentials, degrees, or pedigree. What this means for your resume:
- **Lead with what you can do**, not where you went to school. Skills sections and accomplishment-driven bullets matter more than ever.
- **Show evidence of skill**: Don't just list "Python" — show what you built with it, the scale, and the outcome.
- **Certifications and projects carry real weight** for career changers and non-traditional candidates. Treat them as first-class resume sections.
- **Remove credential gatekeeping from your own thinking**: If the JD says "or equivalent experience," take that seriously.

### The Full Picture: Beyond the Resume
Your resume is one artifact in a broader hiring signal. Hiring managers and recruiters also evaluate:
- **LinkedIn profile**: Recruiters check this before (and after) reading your resume. Align your headline, summary, and experience with your resume narrative.
- **Your professional story**: How you talk about your career in conversations, networking, and interviews reinforces or undermines what's on paper.
- **Reputation and referrals**: Internal referrals and how others describe your work often carry more weight than any document.

Treat the resume as the anchor of a consistent professional narrative — not the entire narrative.

### Using AI Tools Strategically
AI can accelerate your resume work, but it must be used with guardrails:
- **Audit your own resume with AI** before submitting: paste your resume and the JD into a tool and ask it to identify keyword gaps, weak bullets, and formatting issues.
- **Use AI to generate bullet point drafts**, then rewrite them in your voice with your real numbers. AI output without your specifics reads as hollow.
- **Never let AI fabricate experience**. Hiring managers are increasingly skilled at spotting AI-generated fluff, and misrepresentation is a fireable offense even after you're hired.
- **Cross-reference AI suggestions** against the actual JD — AI tools sometimes hallucinate requirements that don't exist.

### Optimize for Humans and Machines
AI and ATS get you past the filter. A human makes the final decision. Design for both:
- **Machine layer**: Standard headings, mirrored JD keywords, clean formatting, parseable structure.
- **Human layer**: Compelling narrative, specific achievements, readability, personality where appropriate (especially in the summary).
- **Test**: If your resume reads like a keyword list, it fails the human layer. If it reads like a personal essay, it fails the machine layer. Hit both.

## Professional Summary

### Purpose
The summary replaces the outdated objective statement. It's a 2–4 sentence pitch that tells the reader: who you are, what you specialize in, and what you bring.

### Formula
**[Title/Level] with [X years] of experience in [domain]. Specialized in [2–3 key skills]. Track record of [outcome]. Seeking to [what you want to do next] at [type of company/role].**

### Common Summary Mistakes
- Too generic: "Hardworking professional with excellent communication skills"
- Too long: More than 5 lines
- Mentions what you want instead of what you offer
- Uses first person ("I am a...")
- Repeats the job title verbatim without adding context

## Career Narrative

### Your Resume Tells a Story
Each role on your resume should not exist in isolation. When read top to bottom, your experience section should show a coherent arc: skills building on each other, scope expanding, and a consistent professional theme emerging.

### Making the Narrative Visible
- **Thread a consistent theme** across roles. If you're a "builder who scales teams and systems," each position should demonstrate a facet of that identity.
- **Show progression**: increasing team size, larger budgets, broader scope, harder problems. Even lateral moves should show depth gained.
- **Connect the dots for the reader**: if a transition between roles isn't obvious (e.g., marketing to product management), use your bullet points to highlight the transferable skills that bridge the two.

### Addressing Gaps Proactively
- Brief parenthetical explanations work well: "Career break (2022–2023): Full-time caregiver" or "Sabbatical (2021): Completed AWS Solutions Architect certification"
- Frame gaps as intentional transitions — education, caregiving, health, or deliberate exploration — rather than leaving them unexplained for the reader to fill in negatively.
- Keep gap explanations to one line. Do not over-explain or apologize.

### Connection to Personal Branding
Your resume narrative is one expression of your core professional story. The same thread should appear in your LinkedIn summary, your elevator pitch, and your interview answers. Consistency across these surfaces builds credibility; contradictions erode it.

## Common Resume Mistakes

### Killer Mistakes (automatic rejection risk)
- Typos or grammatical errors in the top third of the page
- Missing contact information or broken LinkedIn URL
- Employment dates that don't add up (gaps not addressed)
- Inappropriate email address (e.g., partyking@...)
- Photo included (illegal to consider in US hiring; creates liability)
- Salary history included (do not include)

### Major Issues (significantly hurts chances)
- No quantification — every bullet is a duty, not an achievement
- Generic summary that could belong to anyone
- Skills section lists soft skills only ("communication", "leadership")
- Outdated experience taking up space (roles 10+ years old, 3+ bullets each)
- Inconsistent formatting (mixed fonts, uneven spacing, random bold)
- Job title doesn't match industry standard — use common titles ATS recognizes

### Minor Issues (worth fixing)
- Passive language ("was responsible for" instead of "led")
- Orphan lines (single word on its own line)
- Full address included (city, state is sufficient)
- References available upon request (never include — it's assumed)
- Objective statement instead of summary

## Resume Scoring Rubric

Use this when auditing. Score each category 1–10.

| Category | Weight | What to Assess |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| Format & Readability | 15% | Clean layout, consistent spacing, appropriate length |
| ATS Compatibility | 20% | No parsing blockers, keywords present, standard headings |
| Impact Language | 25% | Action verbs, quantification, specificity |
| Relevance to Target Role | 25% | Content matches target role requirements |
| Professional Summary | 15% | Targeted, concise, compelling |

**Final Score = Weighted average of all categories**

Score guide:
- 9–10: Ready to submit
- 7–8: Minor refinements needed
- 5–6: Significant rewrites required
- Below 5: Consider rebuilding from scratch
