# Skill: Cover Letter Writing

## Purpose
Use this skill to draft targeted, compelling cover letters. Apply these frameworks to write letters that are read, not skimmed — and that give candidates an edge beyond their resume.

---

## The State of Cover Letters

Cover letters are polarizing. Some hiring teams don't read them. Others use them as the primary screen. The safest approach: always include one unless the application explicitly says not to. A great cover letter has no downside. A missing one can cost you.

The biggest mistake candidates make: writing a cover letter that summarizes the resume. Hiring managers already have the resume. A cover letter should add context, show personality, and answer the unspoken question: *Why this role, at this company, right now?*

---

## Cover Letter Fundamentals

### Length
- **Target**: 3–4 paragraphs, 250–400 words
- **Maximum**: One page
- Never shorter than 150 words (signals low effort)
- Never longer than one page (signals poor communication skills)

### Format
- Standard business letter format or clean modern layout
- Match the visual style of your resume (same font family)
- Header: Your name + contact info (same as resume header)
- Salutation: "Dear [Hiring Manager's Name]," — research the actual name. If unavailable, use "Dear [Team Name] Hiring Team," (e.g., "Dear Product Hiring Team,"). Never "To Whom It May Concern."
- No "I am writing to apply for..." — this is the most overused opener in existence

## The Four-Paragraph Structure

### Paragraph 1 — The Hook (2–4 sentences)
**Purpose**: Grab attention. Answer: Why this company? Why now?

This is not where you list your credentials. It's where you demonstrate that you actually know and care about this specific company.

**What to include:**
- A genuine observation about the company's work, mission, product, or recent news
- A specific reason why *this* opportunity excites you (not just the job category)
- Something that signals you've done your research

**What to avoid:**
- "I am excited to apply for..."
- Mentioning the job title in the first sentence
- Generic statements about your passion for the industry

**Example:**
> "When [Company] launched [specific product/initiative] earlier this year, I spent an embarrassing amount of time reading everything I could find about the approach. The way your team [specific observation] is exactly the kind of problem I've been obsessed with in my own work at [Current Company]."

---

### Paragraph 2 — The Proof (4–6 sentences)
**Purpose**: Demonstrate you can do the job. Show your most relevant achievement(s).

Pick 1–2 stories or achievements that directly map to the role's most critical requirements. Be specific. Include numbers where possible. This is not a list of responsibilities — it's evidence.

**Structure for each story:**
[Context] → [What you did] → [Result that the hiring manager cares about]

**Example:**
> "In my current role at [Company], I inherited a customer onboarding process with a 34% drop-off rate in the first 30 days. Over six months, I redesigned the in-app experience and built a proactive outreach sequence that reduced first-month churn to 12% and increased expansion revenue from new accounts by 41%. I'd bring this same approach — root-cause diagnosis before solution-building — to your customer success function."

---

### Paragraph 3 — The Fit (2–4 sentences)
**Purpose**: Connect your experience to their specific context. Answer: Why are you the right person for *this* version of this role?

Reference something specific about the company — their stage, their team, their product challenge. Show that you understand what they need and that your background maps to it.

**What to reference:**
- Company stage (early-stage, scaling, enterprise)
- Specific challenge or initiative mentioned in the JD or recent news
- Team culture signals from the job posting
- Something you learned from a contact at the company (if applicable)

**Example:**
> "I know from your job description that you're scaling from 50 to 200 enterprise accounts over the next 18 months — a transition I've lived through twice. The playbook for that scale requires different muscle than the growth-stage scrappiness that got you here, and that's exactly where my background in operationalizing success teams sits."

### Addressing Objections in Paragraph 3

Paragraph 3 is also your opportunity to proactively address the thing a hiring manager might question about your candidacy. Don't wait for them to wonder — name it, reframe it, and move on.

- **Career changers**: "My path from [X] to [Y] wasn't a detour — [specific skill] transfers directly because [concrete connection]." Connect roles through outcomes, not just skills.
- **Employment gaps**: Brief, confident framing — "After [reason], I'm energized to bring [specific value]." One sentence, no apology, no over-explanation.
- **Industry switches**: Connect the dots between industries with transferable *outcomes*, not just transferable skills. "Scaling onboarding in healthcare taught me the same retention mechanics your ed-tech platform needs" is stronger than "I have transferable communication skills."
- **Overqualification**: Emphasize what specifically excites you about this scope rather than explaining away your seniority. "Leading a 50-person org taught me that the work I love most is [thing this role does]" beats "I'm happy to take a step back."

**The rule**: Address it once, confidently, then move on. The more space you give an objection, the bigger it looks. One or two sentences signals awareness; a full paragraph signals insecurity.

---

### Paragraph 4 — The Close (2–3 sentences)
**Purpose**: Express genuine interest, invite next steps, and exit cleanly.

**What to include:**
- A clear expression of interest in speaking further
- A brief forward-looking statement
- A confident close (not "I hope to hear from you")

**What to avoid:**
- Desperation: "I would be so grateful for even a brief conversation"
- Overconfidence: "I know I'm the perfect candidate"
- Demanding: "Please contact me by [date]"
- Filler: "Thank you for your time and consideration" as the only closing line

**Example:**
> "I'd love to talk through how my experience maps to where [Company] is headed. I'm available for a conversation at your convenience and can be reached at [email] or [phone]."

## Storytelling & Career Narrative

Your cover letter is a narrative, not a document summary. The reader should feel a story unfolding — one with a clear beginning (where you've been), middle (what you've proven), and end (where you're going with *them*).

### The Through-Line

What connects your past roles to this opportunity should be a clear, intentional thread. Before you draft a single paragraph, write your through-line sentence:

> "I've spent my career [theme]."

Examples: "I've spent my career making complex products feel simple." / "I've spent my career building systems that scale without breaking the customer experience."

This sentence becomes the backbone of the entire letter. The hook introduces your theme, the proof demonstrates it with evidence, the fit projects it forward into this role.

### Show Progression, Not Just Experience

Each career move should feel intentional — building toward this moment — even if it wasn't planned that way at the time. Frame transitions as chapters in a coherent story, not disconnected job changes.

- Use your core narrative (from personal branding work) as the backbone: who you are, what drives you, and how this role is the natural next step
- The reader should finish the letter thinking "this person's entire career has been pointing toward this role"
- Avoid listing roles chronologically — that's what the resume does. Instead, show how each experience sharpened the specific capability this role demands

**Practical tip**: Write the through-line sentence first, then build the four paragraphs around it. If a paragraph doesn't serve the through-line, cut it or reshape it until it does.

## Tailoring Checklist

Before finalizing a cover letter, verify:

- [ ] Correct company name, role title, and hiring manager name
- [ ] Paragraph 1 references something specific about *this* company (not generic)
- [ ] Paragraph 2 achievement directly maps to a JD requirement
- [ ] No sentences that begin with "I" back-to-back (vary sentence structure)
- [ ] No use of "I am writing to apply for..."
- [ ] Under 400 words
- [ ] Proofread once for typos, once specifically for name/company errors
- [ ] Saved as PDF unless otherwise specified

## AI Screening Considerations

Some companies now use AI to pre-screen cover letters alongside resumes. Your letter needs to pass both a human reader and an automated parser.

- **Keep formatting clean**: No tables, images, unusual characters, or creative layouts that confuse parsers. Stick to plain paragraphs with standard punctuation.
- **Include role-specific keywords naturally**: The same ATS principles from resume writing apply here. Weave in terminology from the job description — but only where it reads naturally.
- **Avoid gimmicks**: Creative formatting, unconventional structures, or embedded links might catch a human's eye but can trip automated screeners.
- **The four-paragraph structure is inherently AI-friendly**: It's clear, parseable, and keyword-rich by design. You don't need a separate "AI-optimized" version — a well-written, clearly structured cover letter passes both AI and human screens.

**Bottom line**: Write for humans, format for machines. If your letter is clear, specific, and well-structured, it will score well with both audiences.

## Tone Guide by Company Type

| Company Type | Tone to Strike |
|-------------|---------------|
| Early-stage startup | Energetic, builder mentality, casual-professional |
| Growth-stage scaleup | Ambitious, data-aware, process-minded |
| Enterprise / Fortune 500 | Polished, stakeholder-aware, structured |
| Nonprofit / mission-driven | Values-forward, impact-oriented, genuine |
| Creative / agency | Voice-forward, show don't tell, personality |
| Technical / engineering | Precise, credibility-first, jargon where appropriate |

## Cover Letter Anti-Patterns

These patterns signal low effort or misunderstanding of what cover letters are for:

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| "I am a hardworking, detail-oriented professional..." | Generic, unprovable, says nothing |
| Restating every bullet on the resume | Redundant — the reader has the resume |
| Mentioning you saw the role on LinkedIn/Indeed | Irrelevant and signals no inside connection |
| Starting every sentence with "I" | Reads as self-centered and is grammatically monotonous |
| Ending with "I look forward to hearing from you" only | Weak close — add a forward-looking statement |
| Generic opener: "I am excited to apply for the [X] role at [Y]" | Used by every candidate — stand out from word one |
| Writing about why you need the job | The letter is about what you offer, not what you need |

## Quick Cover Letter Template

Use this as a starting point, then customize heavily:

---

[Your Name]
[Email] | [Phone] | [LinkedIn]

[Date]

Dear [Name / Team Hiring Team],

[HOOK: Something specific about the company, product, or mission that genuinely resonates with you. Show you've done your homework. 2–3 sentences.]

[PROOF: Your most relevant achievement, told as a mini-story with context and quantified result. Map it directly to the JD's top requirement. 3–5 sentences.]

[FIT: Connect your background to their specific situation — stage, challenge, or initiative. Show you understand what *this* version of the role needs. 2–3 sentences.]

I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my experience can contribute to [Company]'s work on [specific initiative/goal]. I'm available at your convenience — feel free to reach me at [email] or [phone].

[Your Name]

## Cover Letter Length Calibration

| Situation | Recommended Length |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Standard application | 250–350 words |
| Career change (needs more explanation) | 350–450 words |
| Executive/senior role | 300–400 words |
| Referral / personal connection | 150–250 words (shorter is fine) |
| Creative role (portfolio does the work) | 150–250 words |
