# Skill: Interview Preparation

## Purpose
Use this skill when a user has an interview scheduled, wants to practice responses, or needs to understand different interview formats. Apply these frameworks to help candidates walk in prepared, confident, and able to tell their story under pressure.

Two modes: **interactive prep** (walk through exercises, run mock questions) when the user requests practice, and **passive reference** (apply these principles silently) when advising on interview-related questions.

---

## Interview Formats

### Behavioral Interviews
**What they test**: How you've handled real situations in the past. The premise is that past behavior predicts future behavior.

**How to prepare**: Build a story bank (see below) and practice mapping stories to common prompts. Every answer should follow the STAR method. Expect 4-6 behavioral questions in a 45-minute round.

**Typical structure**: The interviewer asks "Tell me about a time when..." and expects a specific, real example — not a hypothetical. Follow-up questions will probe for detail, so vague answers get exposed quickly.

### Case-Based Interviews
**What they test**: Structured problem-solving, business judgment, and your ability to think out loud.

**Common in**: Consulting, product management, strategy, and operations roles.

**How to structure answers**: State your approach before diving in. Break the problem into components. Use rough math confidently. Summarize your recommendation at the end. The interviewer cares more about your reasoning process than the "right" answer.

### Technical Interviews
**What they test**: Domain expertise — coding ability, system design thinking, or domain-specific knowledge.

**Formats vary**: Live coding challenges, whiteboard design sessions, take-home projects, or technical deep-dives on past work. Ask the recruiter what format to expect so you can prepare accordingly.

**Key principle**: Narrate your thinking. Interviewers want to see how you approach problems, not just whether you reach the answer. Silence is your enemy — talk through your reasoning.

### Panel Interviews
**What they test**: The same competencies as other formats, but with multiple evaluators watching simultaneously.

**How dynamics differ**: You'll need to make eye contact and direct answers to the person who asked, while periodically engaging others. Panelists often divide question topics, so expect a wider range of themes. Ask for panelists' names and roles in advance so you can tailor examples.

### Phone Screens
**Recruiter screens** (15-30 min): Verify basic qualifications, salary expectations, timeline, and logistics. Be concise. The recruiter is checking boxes — help them check yours quickly.

**Hiring manager screens** (30-45 min): Deeper conversation about your experience and fit. Treat this as a real interview — it is one. Have your story bank ready and specific questions about the role prepared.

## The STAR Method

### The Framework
Every behavioral answer should follow this structure, with these approximate proportions:

| Component | Weight | What to Include |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Situation** | ~15% | Set the scene briefly. Company, team, and context — just enough so the story makes sense. |
| **Task** | ~10% | What was your specific responsibility or challenge? One sentence is usually enough. |
| **Action** | ~50% | What did *you* do? This is the core. Be specific about your decisions, steps, and reasoning. Use "I" not "we." |
| **Result** | ~25% | What happened? Quantify wherever possible. Include what you learned if the result wasn't perfect. |

Total answer length: 90 seconds to 2 minutes. Practice with a timer.

### Common STAR Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Spending 60% on Situation | Interviewer zones out before you reach the point | Cut context to 2-3 sentences max |
| Saying "we" throughout Action | Interviewer can't tell what *you* did | Replace every "we" with "I" and specify your contribution |
| Vague Action ("I helped improve the process") | No evidence of real work or judgment | Name the specific steps you took and decisions you made |
| Missing or vague Result | Story has no payoff | Always include a number, outcome, or lesson learned |
| Choosing a weak story | Doesn't demonstrate the competency being tested | Map stories to competencies in advance (see Story Bank) |

### STAR Examples

**Prompt**: "Tell me about a time you had to influence someone without authority."

**Weak response**: "I worked on a cross-functional project and convinced the other team to adopt our approach. It went well and we shipped on time."

Problems: No specifics, "we" throughout, no quantified result, impossible to evaluate.

**Strong response**: "At [Company], our data pipeline team needed the platform team to prioritize an API change, but it wasn't on their roadmap. I scheduled a 20-minute session with their tech lead, brought usage data showing the current workaround was costing us 6 hours of engineering time per week, and proposed a phased approach that fit within their sprint capacity. They agreed to the first phase within two weeks. The API change shipped in the next quarter and eliminated 300+ hours of annual manual work across three teams."

Why it works: Specific context, clear individual actions, quantified result, and shows the *how* behind the influence.

**Prompt**: "Describe a time you failed."

**Weak response**: "I'm a perfectionist, so I sometimes take too long on things, but I've gotten better at managing my time."

Problems: This is a disguised strength, not a real failure. Interviewers see through it immediately.

**Strong response**: "I led a product launch that missed its revenue target by 40% in Q1. I had pushed to skip customer validation research because we were behind schedule. After the launch, I ran the research we should have done upfront and discovered a pricing misalignment — our target segment wouldn't pay above $25/month and we'd priced at $39. I rebuilt the pricing model, we re-launched at $22 with an annual option, and hit 110% of the revised target in Q3. The lesson stuck: I now build validation checkpoints into every launch plan, even when timelines are tight."

Why it works: Real failure, takes ownership, shows specific corrective action, and demonstrates lasting behavioral change.

## Building Your Story Bank

### Why You Need One
Interviews reward preparation, not improvisation. When you're under pressure, you won't magically recall the perfect example. You'll reach for whatever comes to mind first — which is rarely your strongest story. A story bank gives you pre-selected, pre-structured answers ready to deploy.

### The 8 Story Categories
Prepare at least one strong STAR story for each of these categories. Most stories can map to 2-3 categories.

1. **Leadership** — Guiding a team, making tough calls, setting direction
2. **Conflict resolution** — Disagreement with a colleague, stakeholder, or manager
3. **Failure and learning** — Something that went wrong and what you took from it
4. **Collaboration** — Working across teams, functions, or with difficult partners
5. **Initiative and ownership** — Seeing a problem and fixing it without being asked
6. **Problem-solving** — Diagnosing a complex issue and finding a creative solution
7. **Time pressure** — Delivering under a tight deadline or shifting priorities
8. **Growth and learning** — Acquiring a new skill, adapting to a new domain

### Interactive Exercise: Map Your Stories
Let's build your story bank together:

1. **List 8-10 professional experiences** that stand out — moments where you drove an outcome, overcame a challenge, or learned something significant.
2. **Tag each story** to one or more of the 8 categories above. Flag any category with zero stories — that's a gap to fill.
3. **Draft each story in STAR format.** Keep each under 2 minutes when spoken aloud.
4. **Stress-test coverage**: Can you answer questions about leadership, failure, conflict, and collaboration without reusing a story? If not, add more stories to thin categories.

*Goal: 8-10 stories that cover all 8 categories, with no single story doing triple duty.*

## Common Question Frameworks

### "Tell me about yourself"
This is not an invitation to recite your resume. It is a 2-minute pitch structured as:

**Present** (30 sec): What you do now and what you're focused on.
**Past** (45 sec): The 1-2 experiences that are most relevant to *this role*.
**Future** (30 sec): Why you're here — what draws you to this opportunity.

Keep it under 2 minutes. Practice until it feels natural, not rehearsed. Tailor the "past" section to emphasize whatever is most relevant to the specific role.

### "Why this company?"
The interviewer is testing whether you've done your homework and have genuine interest. Generic answers ("I love the mission") fail this test.

**Formula**: Genuine observation + connection to your experience + forward-looking fit.

> "I've been following [Company]'s approach to [specific initiative] since [specific moment]. In my current role, I've worked on [related problem], so I understand both the difficulty and the opportunity. I'm drawn to this role because [specific aspect of the job] maps directly to where I want to take my career."

### "What's your greatest weakness?"
Give a real weakness. Describe what you've done to manage it. Never use a disguised strength ("I work too hard").

> "I tend to over-research before making decisions, which has slowed me down on time-sensitive projects. I've built a personal rule: if a decision is reversible, I give myself a maximum of one day to gather data before committing. It's made me significantly faster without sacrificing quality on the decisions that actually matter."

### Behavioral Prompts
For any "Tell me about a time when..." question:

1. Identify which category it maps to (leadership, conflict, failure, etc.)
2. Pull the pre-prepared story from your story bank
3. Deliver it in STAR format, emphasizing the Action
4. Land the Result with a number or concrete outcome
5. If there's a learning component, state it in one sentence at the end

## Pre-Interview Research Checklist

Complete this research for every interview. Showing up uninformed is the fastest way to get rejected.

**About the company:**
- [ ] What does the company do? (Product/service, customers, business model)
- [ ] What stage is the company at? (Startup, growth, mature, turnaround)
- [ ] Recent news — funding rounds, product launches, leadership changes, earnings
- [ ] Company mission and values (from their website, not Glassdoor)

**About the role:**
- [ ] Key responsibilities and required skills from the JD
- [ ] What team does this role sit on? Who does it report to?
- [ ] Why is this role open? (New headcount vs. backfill — ask the recruiter)

**About the people:**
- [ ] Look up each interviewer on LinkedIn — their background, tenure, role
- [ ] Note any shared connections, interests, or experiences

**About the culture:**
- [ ] Glassdoor reviews (look for patterns across reviews, not individual complaints)
- [ ] Company engineering blog, social media, or public talks by employees
- [ ] Interview process reviews on Glassdoor or Blind

**Prepare to ask:**
- [ ] 3-5 thoughtful questions that demonstrate your research and genuine curiosity
- [ ] At least one question about the team's biggest current challenge

## Post-Interview Follow-Up

### Thank You Notes
**Timing**: Within 24 hours. Same-day is better.

**Format**: Email, unless the interviewer specifically mentioned another preference.

**What to include:**
- Thank them by name for their time
- Reference one specific topic from the conversation (proves you were engaged)
- Briefly reinforce your fit for one key requirement
- Keep it under 150 words — this is a note, not a second cover letter

**Template:**
> Subject: Thank you — [Role Title] conversation
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for taking the time to speak with me about the [Role Title] position. I particularly enjoyed our discussion about [specific topic] — it reinforced my excitement about contributing to [specific goal or initiative].
>
> After learning more about [specific challenge or project], I'm confident my experience with [relevant skill/achievement] would translate well to this role.
>
> I look forward to the next steps. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you need any additional information.
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]

### Timeline Expectations

| Company Type | Typical Decision Timeline |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| Startup (< 50 people) | 3-7 days |
| Growth-stage (50-500) | 1-2 weeks |
| Enterprise / Fortune 500 | 2-4 weeks (sometimes longer) |
| Government / academia | 4-8 weeks |

### Following Up
If you haven't heard back by the expected timeline:

**First follow-up** (1 week past expected date): Brief, professional, expressing continued interest. One email, addressed to the recruiter or hiring manager.

> "Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up on my interview for [Role] on [date]. I remain very interested in the opportunity and would welcome any updates on the timeline. Happy to provide any additional information that would be helpful."

**Second follow-up** (2 weeks after first): Only if you received no response to the first. Keep it even shorter. If there's still no response after this, move on — the silence is the answer.
