# Skill: Self-Discovery & Career Clarity

## Purpose
Use this skill when a user doesn't know what role they want, is considering a career change, feels unhappy or stuck in their current role, or is entering the workforce for the first time. This skill operates in two modes:

- **Interactive mode**: Claude walks the user through exercises step by step, asking questions and processing answers before moving on.
- **Passive reference mode**: Claude draws on these frameworks silently to inform advice, role suggestions, and resume strategy without explicitly running the exercises.

---

## Career Stage Assessment

Every user is at a different point in their career journey. Identifying their stage changes what advice matters most.

### The Five Stages

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Discover** | Exploring options, researching paths, no concrete direction yet. Asking "what could I do?" |
| **Prepare** | Building skills, gaining experience, working toward a specific direction. Asking "how do I get ready?" |
| **Establish** | In a role and building traction. Focused on proving competence and growing within a defined path. |
| **Advance** | Performing well and seeking the next level — leadership, specialization, or expanded scope. |
| **Navigate** | Reassessing direction after a significant shift — layoff, burnout, industry change, or life transition. |

### Interactive Exercise: Identify Your Stage

Walk the user through these five questions, one at a time. Use their answers to place them in a stage.

1. Do you have a clear idea of what type of work you want to do? (Yes / Somewhat / No)
2. Have you held a role in your target field for more than 6 months? (Yes / No)
3. Do you feel competent and respected in your current or most recent role? (Yes / No / Not applicable)
4. Are you actively seeking growth — promotions, new challenges, or bigger scope? (Yes / No)
5. Have you recently experienced a major disruption — layoff, burnout, career doubt, or life change? (Yes / No)

**Scoring logic:**
- Q1 = No --> **Discover**
- Q1 = Somewhat, Q2 = No --> **Prepare**
- Q2 = Yes, Q3 = No --> **Establish** (still building footing)
- Q2 = Yes, Q3 = Yes, Q4 = Yes --> **Advance**
- Q5 = Yes (regardless of other answers) --> **Navigate**

### How Stage Shapes Downstream Advice

| Stage | Resume Priority | Job Search Focus | Key Risk |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|----------|
| Discover | Transferable skills, projects, education | Informational interviews, exploration | Paralysis from too many options |
| Prepare | Skills-based format, certifications, relevant projects | Entry-level roles, internships, portfolio building | Waiting too long to start applying |
| Establish | Achievement-driven bullets, measurable results | Targeted applications, building reputation | Staying too comfortable, not documenting wins |
| Advance | Leadership impact, business outcomes, strategic scope | Networking up, executive recruiters, speaking/writing | Optimizing for title instead of actual fit |
| Navigate | Reframing narrative, bridging transferable skills | Rebuilding network, exploring adjacent paths | Rushing into the next thing without reflecting |

## Values Clarity

### Why Values Matter

Your values are the filter for every career decision — which roles to pursue, which offers to accept, which companies to avoid. When your work aligns with your values, you perform better and stay longer. When it doesn't, even a great title and salary won't prevent dissatisfaction.

Most people skip this step and optimize for compensation or prestige alone. That works until it doesn't. Getting clear on values now saves you from accepting the wrong role six months from now.

Values also evolve. What mattered at 22 (stability, income) often shifts by 30 (autonomy, impact). Revisiting this periodically is normal and healthy.

### Values List

Here are 20 career-relevant values. Each one has real implications for the types of roles and companies that will fit you.

| Value | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **Achievement** | Producing measurable results and hitting ambitious goals |
| **Autonomy** | Having control over how, when, and where you work |
| **Balance** | Maintaining clear boundaries between work and personal life |
| **Challenge** | Solving hard problems that push your abilities |
| **Collaboration** | Working closely with others toward shared outcomes |
| **Compensation** | Earning a salary and benefits that reflect your contribution |
| **Creativity** | Generating original ideas and building new things |
| **Growth** | Continuously learning and developing new capabilities |
| **Impact** | Seeing your work make a tangible difference in the world |
| **Independence** | Working with minimal oversight or bureaucracy |
| **Innovation** | Operating at the frontier of new ideas and technologies |
| **Leadership** | Guiding, mentoring, and influencing others |
| **Mission** | Working for an organization whose purpose you believe in |
| **Prestige** | Being associated with respected brands and institutions |
| **Recognition** | Having your contributions acknowledged and rewarded |
| **Security** | Having stable employment, predictable income, and low risk |
| **Service** | Helping individuals directly through your work |
| **Structure** | Operating within clear processes, expectations, and roles |
| **Variety** | Doing different types of work across projects and contexts |
| **Ownership** | Having direct responsibility and accountability for outcomes |

### Interactive Exercise: Find Your Top 5

Walk the user through this step by step. Do not skip steps.

**Step 1**: Present the full list of 20 values above. Ask the user to pick 10 that resonate most.

**Step 2**: From their 10, ask them to eliminate 5. Frame it as: "If you could only keep 5 of these in your next role, which would they be?" Push back if they try to keep more — the constraint is the point.

**Step 3**: Ask them to rank their final 5 from most to least important.

**Step 4**: For each of their top 5, share what it means for their job search using the translation table below.

## Identity & Fit Diagnostic

### The Three Types of Career Unhappiness

Not all career dissatisfaction is the same. Misdiagnosing the problem leads to the wrong solution — like quitting a job when you actually need a vacation, or taking a vacation when you actually need a new career.

**1. Burnout — You need rest, not a new job.**

Symptoms: Exhaustion even after weekends. Cynicism about work you used to enjoy. Physical symptoms — sleep issues, headaches, irritability. Dreading Monday but not because the work itself is wrong.

Solution: Before making any career moves, recover. Take PTO, set boundaries, reduce workload. If you still feel this way after genuine rest, then reassess the role.

**2. Boredom — You need growth, not an exit.**

Symptoms: You can do the job easily but feel unchallenged. You're coasting. You daydream about projects outside work. You haven't learned anything new in months.

Solution: Seek stretch opportunities in your current role first — new projects, lateral moves, mentorship, skill-building. A bored person in the right field just needs a harder version of the same work.

**3. Misfit — You're in the wrong role entirely.**

Symptoms: The core activities of your job drain you regardless of workload. You feel out of place with your peers. Your strengths go unused. No amount of rest or challenge changes the feeling.

Solution: This is the one that requires a real change — different role type, different function, possibly different industry. Use the values and work style exercises in this skill to identify what actually fits.

### Interactive Exercise: Diagnose Your Situation

Ask these five questions one at a time. Tally the responses.

1. When you imagine doing your current work with half the workload, does it sound appealing? (Yes = Burnout / No = Not burnout)
2. When you imagine doing your current work at twice the complexity, does it sound exciting? (Yes = Boredom / No = Not boredom)
3. Do you admire the people who are great at your job, or does their success feel irrelevant to you? (Irrelevant = Misfit / Admire = Not misfit)
4. If you could redesign your current role from scratch, would you keep the core function? (Yes = Burnout or Boredom / No = Misfit)
5. When you talk about your work, do you feel drained by the substance or just the volume? (Volume = Burnout / Substance = Misfit / Neither, it's too easy = Boredom)

**Interpretation:**
- Majority Burnout answers --> Recommend recovery before any career moves. Flag that job searching while burned out leads to poor decisions.
- Majority Boredom answers --> Recommend internal growth strategies: stretch projects, lateral moves, skill stacking. Only explore external roles if the current company has no growth path.
- Majority Misfit answers --> Recommend the full self-discovery workflow: values exercise, work style assessment, and target role profiling before applying anywhere.

## Work Style Preferences

### Six Work Style Archetypes

These are simplified from the RIASEC personality framework used in career psychology. Most people are a blend of two or three. Identifying your top types helps filter roles and environments.

**Builder**
Hands-on, practical, results-oriented. Prefers doing over discussing. Thrives in environments with tangible outputs — products shipped, systems built, problems fixed. Drained by abstract meetings, excessive planning, and politics.
Example roles: Software Engineer, Mechanical Engineer, Construction Manager, Lab Technician

**Thinker**
Analytical, curious, independent. Prefers deep work on complex problems. Thrives in environments that reward expertise and intellectual rigor. Drained by small talk, surface-level work, and constant context-switching.
Example roles: Data Scientist, Research Analyst, Strategy Consultant, Economist

**Creator**
Imaginative, expressive, non-conformist. Prefers open-ended work with creative freedom. Thrives in environments that value originality and aesthetics. Drained by rigid processes, repetitive tasks, and heavy bureaucracy.
Example roles: UX Designer, Content Strategist, Brand Director, Architect

**Helper**
Empathetic, patient, people-oriented. Prefers work that directly improves others' lives. Thrives in environments built around relationships and service. Drained by impersonal systems, pure metrics focus, and isolation.
Example roles: Therapist, Teacher, Customer Success Manager, Nurse, Social Worker

**Leader**
Persuasive, competitive, decisive. Prefers influence, strategy, and driving outcomes. Thrives in environments with clear goals, visible impact, and upward mobility. Drained by ambiguity, slow decision-making, and lack of authority.
Example roles: Product Manager, Sales Director, Founder, Management Consultant

**Organizer**
Detail-oriented, reliable, systematic. Prefers clear processes and structured work. Thrives in environments where accuracy and consistency matter. Drained by chaos, undefined roles, and constant pivoting.
Example roles: Operations Manager, Accountant, Project Manager, Compliance Analyst

### How to Use Your Work Style

After identifying a user's top 2 archetypes (through conversation or the diagnostic), connect them to search strategy:

| Top Archetype | Best Search Channels | Company Types to Target | How to Evaluate Roles |
|--------------|---------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Builder | Technical job boards, GitHub, referrals | Engineering-driven orgs, product companies | Look for: build vs. maintain ratio, tech stack, shipping cadence |
| Thinker | Academic networks, niche communities, research orgs | R&D-heavy companies, think tanks, data teams | Look for: depth of work, independence, publication/thought leadership |
| Creator | Portfolio-based platforms, design communities, agencies | Creative agencies, brand-forward companies, startups | Look for: creative freedom, portfolio potential, design maturity |
| Helper | Mission-driven boards, nonprofit networks, education orgs | Nonprofits, healthcare, ed-tech, customer-centric companies | Look for: direct user/client contact, mission alignment, team culture |
| Leader | Executive recruiters, LinkedIn, industry events | High-growth startups, sales-driven orgs, consulting firms | Look for: scope of ownership, team size, decision-making authority |
| Organizer | Operations-focused boards, corporate career pages | Established companies, finance, logistics, regulated industries | Look for: process maturity, role clarity, career ladder |

## Translating Self-Knowledge Into Action

### Building Your Target Role Profile

Once a user has completed the values, stage, and work style exercises, synthesize their results into a concrete search profile. Use this template:

```
## Your Target Role Profile

**Career Stage**: [stage] — This means you should prioritize [stage-specific focus].

**Top 5 Values**: [list] — Filter every opportunity against these. If a role conflicts with your top 2, pass on it.

**Work Style**: [top 2 archetypes] — Look for roles and environments that match these energy patterns.

**Target Role Characteristics**:
- Type of work: [derived from work style]
- Environment: [derived from values + work style]
- Company stage/size: [derived from values]
- Must-haves: [from values — non-negotiable]
- Dealbreakers: [red flags from values table]
```

### Evaluating Job Descriptions for Fit

When a user shares a job description, evaluate it against their self-knowledge profile:

**Green flags** (signals of alignment):
- JD language matches their top values (e.g., "autonomy" user sees "self-directed" in description)
- Role activities map to their work style archetype
- Company signals match values translation table
- Growth path matches their career stage needs

**Red flags** (signals of misalignment):
- Core responsibilities conflict with work style (e.g., Creator in a rigid compliance role)
- Company culture contradicts top values (e.g., Balance-focused user, job says "fast-paced, always-on")
- Role is wrong for their career stage (e.g., Discover-stage user applying to senior leadership role)
- JD contains red flags from their values translation table

### Informing Your Resume Narrative

Self-knowledge shapes how a user tells their professional story:

- **Values** determine which accomplishments to highlight. A user who values Impact should lead with outcomes and results. A user who values Creativity should emphasize innovation and original work.
- **Work style** determines framing. A Builder should emphasize what they shipped. A Thinker should emphasize what they analyzed or solved. A Leader should emphasize what they drove and who they influenced.
- **Career stage** determines structure. Discover-stage users benefit from skills-based resumes. Establish-stage users need achievement-heavy chronological formats. Navigate-stage users need a strong narrative bridge connecting their past to their target.

Use these insights when applying the resume-best-practices and resume-customization skills. Self-discovery output feeds directly into resume strategy.
