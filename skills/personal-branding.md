# Skill: Personal Branding

## Purpose
Use this skill when a user needs to develop their professional narrative, optimize their LinkedIn profile, craft an elevator pitch, or audit their digital presence. Apply these frameworks for career changers translating their experience, early-career candidates building a presence from scratch, or experienced professionals sharpening how they show up.

Two modes: **interactive exercises** (walk through prompts, draft pitches together) when the user requests hands-on help, and **passive reference** (apply these principles silently) when advising on resumes, cover letters, or networking.

---

## The Core Narrative

### "You Are Not a Resume"
Your personal brand is not a list of jobs you have held. It is the through-line that connects what you care about, what you have done, and where you are headed. When this narrative is clear, everything else — your resume summary, your LinkedIn bio, your cover letter hook, your answer to "tell me about yourself" — writes itself.

The framework has three parts:

1. **Who you are** — Your identity, values, and the lens you bring to work
2. **What drives you** — The problems you care about and the curiosity that pulls you forward
3. **Where you are headed** — The direction you are moving, not a fixed destination

Most people skip straight to listing skills. That is forgettable. A clear narrative makes people remember you and understand why you do what you do.

### Interactive Exercise: Build Your Core Narrative
Answer these reflection prompts in short phrases — do not overthink them:

| Prompt | What It Reveals |
|--------|----------------|
| What am I genuinely curious about? | Your natural interests and direction |
| What experiences shaped how I think? | The stories that define your perspective |
| What values guide my decisions? | Your non-negotiables and priorities |
| What problem do I care about solving? | Your professional purpose |
| What do I want to do more of? | The work that energizes you |
| How do people describe me? | Your reputation — the brand that already exists |

Now synthesize your answers into three sentences:

> "I am [identity/role] drawn to [curiosity/theme]. My experience in [key experiences] taught me [core insight]. I am focused on [current direction] because [why it matters to you]."

This is your core narrative. Everything below builds on it.

## Elevator Pitch

### Three Versions
You need your pitch at different lengths for different contexts. Each version draws from the same core narrative but adjusts scope and detail.

**30-second pitch (networking events, casual introductions):**
Your identity + what you are passionate about + what you are currently focused on. Keep it conversational. End with something that invites a follow-up question.

**60-second pitch (interviews, informational meetings):**
Your identity + one key experience and what it taught you + your current goal + the problem space you are drawn to. This is your "tell me about yourself" answer.

**Written pitch (LinkedIn about section, email introductions):**
Your story arc + values + current goal + invitation to connect. This version can breathe — use 100-200 words.

### Templates

**Template 1: General Purpose**
"I'm a [your identity or role] who's passionate about [what drives you]. My background in [key experience] taught me [core lesson or strength]. Right now, I'm focused on [what you're doing or aiming for], and I'm especially interested in [problem space or impact area]."

**Template 2: Memorable Moment Story**
"One moment that shaped me was [experience]. It helped me realize [key insight about yourself or the world]. Since then, I've been [what you pursued], and I'm looking to keep growing by [what you're exploring or seeking]."

Best for: Interview openers, networking introductions where you want to stand out. A specific moment is more memorable than a general summary.

**Template 3: Lighthouse Signal (LinkedIn bio / long-form)**
"I'm someone who's always been drawn to [curiosity or theme]. Whether it was [early experience] or [recent role], I've consistently found myself [doing what]. I care deeply about [problem you want to solve] — and I believe [your worldview or thesis]. I'm currently [your role, project, or goal], and I'm always looking to connect with others who [shared values or purpose]."

Best for: LinkedIn about sections, personal websites, speaker bios. This version shows the thread across your career.

### Adapting for Context

| Context | Emphasize | Tone |
|---------|-----------|------|
| Networking | Your curiosity and interests | Conversational, inviting |
| Job interview | Past experience + relevant goals | Confident, specific |
| Resume summary | Key skills + accomplishments | Direct, results-oriented |
| LinkedIn bio | Story arc + values + current goal | Authentic, forward-looking |
| Email introduction | Mutual connection + what you offer | Brief, purposeful |

## LinkedIn Optimization

### Headline Formula
Your headline is the first thing people read and the primary text that shows up in search results. Do not waste it on just your job title.

**Formula**: [Role] | [Specialization] | [Value proposition or interest area]

Examples:
- "Software Engineer | Backend Systems & Data Infrastructure | Building tools that scale"
- "Marketing Manager | B2B SaaS Growth | Turning data into pipeline"
- "Career Changer | Ex-Teacher Pivoting to UX Research | Obsessed with how people learn"

### About Section Structure
The About section is your Lighthouse Signal pitch (Template 3) in action. Structure it as:

1. **Opening hook** — Do not start with "I am a..." Start with what you care about, a question, or a brief story
2. **What you do** — Your current work and expertise, 2-3 sentences
3. **What drives you** — The "why" behind your work
4. **What you are looking for** — Be specific: roles, collaborations, conversations
5. **Call to action** — "Reach out if..." or "I'd love to connect with..."

### Experience Section Tips
- Mirror your resume bullets but write in first person — LinkedIn is more personal
- Lead each role with a 1-2 sentence summary of the role's scope before listing accomplishments
- Add media: presentations, articles, project links. Most people never do this, which makes it easy to stand out
- Use LinkedIn's formatting (bold, bullet points) to improve readability

### Skills & Endorsements
- Pin your top 3 skills — these should match the keywords in your target roles
- Request endorsements strategically from colleagues who have seen you use those skills
- Align skill tags with the language from job descriptions in your target field

### Content Engagement
You do not need to become a LinkedIn influencer. Consistent, low-effort engagement builds visibility.

- **Comment** on industry content 2-3 times per week. Thoughtful comments get more profile views than posts
- **Share** articles with a short take (2-3 sentences on why it matters)
- **Post** original content only when you have something genuine to say — once a month is plenty
- Quality over quantity. One thoughtful comment beats five empty reactions

## Digital Presence Audit

### The Google Test
Search your own name. What shows up on the first page? That is your brand whether you built it intentionally or not.

**What you want to see**: LinkedIn profile, portfolio or personal site, professional mentions, published work.

**What you do not want to see**: Outdated profiles, embarrassing social media, someone else with your name dominating results.

### Platform Checklist

| Platform | Who Needs It | Priority |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| LinkedIn | Everyone | Essential |
| GitHub / GitLab | Technical roles | High for engineers |
| Portfolio site | Designers, writers, marketers | High for creative roles |
| Twitter / X | Media, tech, academia | Medium — depends on field |
| Personal website | Anyone who wants to control their narrative | Recommended |

**Consistency rule**: Use the same professional photo, the same core narrative, and the same keywords across every platform. Inconsistency signals a lack of clarity — or worse, makes you look like two different people.

### What to Clean Up
- Set old social media accounts to private or delete them if they are inactive
- Remove or update profiles on job boards you no longer use
- Google your name + the word "resume" — you may have old versions floating around
- Anything that contradicts your professional brand should be removed or buried under better content

### Interactive Exercise: Audit Your Digital Presence
1. Google your full name (in quotes) and review the first two pages of results
2. Google your name + your target job title — do you show up in relevant contexts?
3. Open every professional profile you have and check: Does each one tell the same story? Same photo? Same keywords?
4. List anything that needs to be updated, removed, or created
5. Prioritize: LinkedIn first, then your most field-relevant platform, then everything else

## Transferable Skills Translation

### Why This Matters
Career changers, industry switchers, and anyone pivoting roles face the same problem: you have valuable skills, but they are dressed in the wrong vocabulary. The experience is real — you just need to translate it into language your target field recognizes.

Transferable skills — communication, problem-solving, leadership, project management, critical thinking — are the foundation of every role. They cross industries and job titles. The candidates who get stuck are the ones who describe their experience in the language of the field they are leaving, not the field they are entering.

### Translation Framework

| What You Did | What It Is Called in the Target Field | How to Phrase It |
|-------------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Managed classroom of 30 students | Stakeholder management, group facilitation | "Facilitated daily sessions for 30+ participants, managing competing needs and maintaining engagement" |
| Handled customer complaints | Client relations, conflict resolution | "Resolved escalated client issues, maintaining a 95% satisfaction rating" |
| Organized volunteer events | Project management, event coordination | "Led cross-functional volunteer teams of 15+ to plan and execute community events" |
| Trained new hires | Learning & development, onboarding | "Designed and delivered onboarding programs that reduced new hire ramp time by 30%" |

### Common Translations
These skills transfer across nearly every industry — they just change names:

- **Customer service** → stakeholder management, client success
- **Teaching / tutoring** → training, enablement, learning design
- **Volunteering / community work** → project management, program coordination
- **Retail / hospitality** → operations, customer experience, team leadership
- **Military service** → logistics, operations management, leadership under pressure
- **Freelancing** → business development, client management, self-direction

### Interactive Exercise: Translate Your Skills
1. List 5 key skills from your current or most recent role — use the language you would naturally use
2. For each skill, identify what it would be called in your target field
3. Rewrite each as a resume bullet using the target field's vocabulary
4. Test it: Would someone in your target field immediately understand what you did and why it matters? If not, revise until they would
