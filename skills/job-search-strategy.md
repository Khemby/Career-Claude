# Skill: Job Search Strategy

## Purpose
Use this skill to help users build a systematic, effective job search. Cover role targeting, sourcing channels, application strategy, networking, and tracking.

---

## Job Search Fundamentals

### The Core Problem Most Candidates Have
Most people spray and pray — they apply to dozens of roles through job boards and wait. This is the least effective strategy. The most effective searches are targeted, networked, and systematic.

**The 70% Rule**: Roughly 70% of jobs are filled through referrals and networking before ever being posted publicly. Competing only on job boards means competing for ~30% of opportunities against everyone else.

---

## Step 1: Define Your Target

Before applying anywhere, get specific about what you're looking for.

### Role Targeting Framework
Answer these questions:
1. **What job titles match what I want to do?** (List 3–5 variations)
2. **What industries am I open to?** (Start broad, then filter)
3. **What company size do I prefer?** (Startup / SMB / Mid-market / Enterprise)
4. **What geography or remote policy do I require?**
5. **What is my minimum acceptable compensation?**
6. **What culture / team environment do I thrive in?**

### Role Title Research
Job titles vary significantly across companies. A "Growth Marketing Manager" at one company is called "Demand Generation Lead" at another. Research 5–10 job postings for roles you want and collect all title variations. Use all of them in your search.

### The Ideal Job Profile
Create a simple doc listing:
- Must-haves (dealbreakers if missing)
- Nice-to-haves (preferred but negotiable)
- Dealbreakers (things you will not accept)

This filters out noise and keeps you from wasting time applying to the wrong roles.

## Step 2: Sourcing Channels

### Tier 1 — Highest Signal (prioritize these)
1. **Your network** — Warm outreach to former colleagues, managers, and contacts. Ask not for a job, but for a conversation. "I'm exploring [role type] opportunities — would you have 20 minutes to share your perspective on [company/industry]?"
2. **LinkedIn — Direct outreach to hiring managers** — Find the person who would be your manager and send a thoughtful connection request + short message. Don't apply blind through LinkedIn's Easy Apply.
3. **Company career pages** — If you have a target company list, go direct. You avoid the noise of job boards and signal genuine interest.

### Tier 2 — High Volume, Lower Signal
4. **LinkedIn Jobs** — Use for discovery and research, not mass application. Keyword search, filter by date posted (last 7 days = freshest).
5. **Indeed** — Best for roles that don't require LinkedIn presence (operations, admin, logistics, trades).
6. **Greenhouse / Lever / Workday job boards** — Many companies post directly to their ATS portal. Use their public job boards.

### Tier 3 — Supplementary
7. **Wellfound (formerly AngelList)** — Best for startup roles
8. **Glassdoor** — Useful for salary and culture research. Application volume is lower than LinkedIn.
9. **Industry-specific boards** — Dice (tech), Mediabistro (media), Idealist (nonprofit), Behance (design), etc.

### Sources to Deprioritize
- ZipRecruiter — High volume, lower quality matches
- Monster — Declining relevance
- Generic "apply in one click" aggregators — High competition, low signal

## Step 3: Evaluating a Role

Before applying, score the role on these dimensions.

### Fit Assessment (1–5 each)
| Dimension | Questions to Ask |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Role Fit** | Does the JD describe work I've done and want to keep doing? |
| **Level Fit** | Is the seniority appropriate? (Stretch by 1 level max) |
| **Company Fit** | Is this an industry/company I'm genuinely interested in? |
| **Culture Fit** | Based on research, does the team/culture match my work style? |
| **Comp Fit** | Does the likely comp range meet my minimum? |

**Apply if score is 18+/25. Research more if 14–17. Skip if below 14.**

### Red Flags in Job Descriptions
- Extremely long requirements list with oddly specific combinations (often a wishlist, not a real role)
- "Wear many hats" with no seniority — often means understaffed and undercompensated
- No salary range listed (in states that require disclosure, this signals non-compliance)
- Posted more than 30 days ago without changes — may already be filled or frozen
- Vague success metrics ("you'll help us grow")

### Green Flags
- Clear scope of responsibility
- Explicit growth path mentioned
- Salary range included
- Recent posting (within 7–14 days)
- Named hiring manager in the posting

## Step 4: Application Strategy

### The Target List Method
Instead of applying to everything, maintain a target list of 20–30 companies you'd genuinely want to work for. Research each one monthly. Apply when a relevant role appears. This keeps your search focused and your materials tailored.

### Application Prioritization
- **Tier A**: Dream companies or perfect-fit roles — invest 2–4 hours (tailored resume + cover letter + network outreach)
- **Tier B**: Good fit roles — invest 45–90 minutes (tailored resume, note to a contact if possible)
- **Tier C**: Reach / exploratory — invest 20–30 minutes (base resume, quick customization)

Never mass-apply with an untailored resume. A focused search with 5 tailored applications beats 50 generic ones.

### Timing Matters
- Best days to apply: Tuesday–Thursday
- Best time: Morning (applications reviewed at start of day)
- Roles posted Friday afternoon often get reviewed Monday — apply over the weekend to be at the top

### Following Up
- Wait 5–7 business days after applying before following up
- One polite follow-up is appropriate. Two is the maximum.
- Follow up with the recruiter or hiring manager via LinkedIn, not through the ATS portal
- Template: "Hi [name], I applied for [role] on [date] and remain very interested. I'd love to connect if you're still reviewing candidates."

## Step 5: Networking Strategy

### Who to Contact
1. **Warm connections** — Former colleagues, managers, classmates, clients
2. **2nd-degree connections** — People connected to your warm contacts at target companies
3. **Alumni networks** — Your college alumni network is an underused resource
4. **LinkedIn cold outreach** — Works when personalized; fails when generic

### The Informational Interview
Request a 20-minute call, not a job. Frame it as seeking advice, not a favor.

**Message Template:**
> "Hi [Name], I came across your profile while researching [company/role type]. I'm a [your background] exploring opportunities in [space]. Would you have 20 minutes for a quick call? I'm specifically curious about [genuine specific question]. Happy to work around your schedule."

Success rate goes up with:
- Personalized opener (reference something specific about them)
- Specific ask (not "pick your brain")
- Easy yes/no framing (20 minutes, not a vague coffee)

### What to Do in the Informational Interview
1. Thank them and reiterate you're there to learn, not ask for a job
2. Ask 3–4 thoughtful questions (research their background first)
3. Listen more than you talk
4. At the end: "Is there anyone else you'd recommend I speak with?"
5. Send a thank you note within 24 hours
6. Add them to your CRM/tracker and stay in touch quarterly

## Step 6: Application Tracking

Track every application. Without a system, the search becomes chaotic.

### Minimum Tracking Fields
| Field | What to Log |
|-------|------------|
| Company | Name + website |
| Role | Title + link to posting (save the JD — it gets taken down) |
| Date Applied | When you submitted |
| Status | Applied / Phone Screen / Interview / Offer / Rejected / Ghosted |
| Contact | Recruiter or hiring manager name + LinkedIn |
| Notes | Key requirements, follow-up dates, anything said in conversations |
| Next Action | What you need to do and by when |

Use a spreadsheet, Notion, or tools like Teal or Huntr. Review your tracker weekly.

## Managing the Search Mentally

### Realistic Timelines
- Entry-level roles: 6–12 weeks average
- Mid-level roles: 8–16 weeks average
- Senior/executive roles: 3–6 months average

Job searching is a numbers game with a long feedback loop. Don't optimize for volume — optimize for quality of activity.

### Weekly Activity Targets (active search)
- 5–10 tailored applications
- 3–5 networking touchpoints (messages, calls, coffee chats)
- 1–2 informational interviews
- 1 skills development activity (course, portfolio piece, writing)

### Handling Rejection
- Most rejections are not about you — they reflect fit, timing, internal candidates, budget changes
- Request feedback when possible (rarely given, but worth asking)
- Stay in contact with companies you liked — roles reopen, teams grow
- Track your conversion rates: applications → screen → interview → offer. If you're getting screens but no interviews, the problem is your pitch. If you're not getting screens, the problem is your application materials.
